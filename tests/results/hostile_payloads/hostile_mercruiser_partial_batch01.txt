### ISSUE #9: Heat Exchanger Blockage / Failure (Closed-Cooling)
**Category:** Cooling System
**Customer Complaint:** The engine gradually overheats — it used to run at 160°F and now it's creeping up to 180-190°F, especially at higher RPMs.

**ANSWER KEY FIX (Ground Truth):**
Remove and inspect or replace the heat exchanger. Salt, mineral deposits, and zinc corrosion products accumulate in the heat exchanger tubes over time, reducing heat transfer efficiency. The heat exchanger can be rodded out (tubes cleaned with a brush and flushed with a mild acid solution followed by thorough rinsing) or replaced entirely if corroded through. Also inspect and replace the zinc pencil anode inside the heat exchanger — this sacrificial zinc protects the exchanger from galvanic corrosion. Replace the zinc anode at the interval specified in the MerCruiser maintenance schedule. Use the correct OEM or equivalent replacement part. Also check the raw-water side of the exchanger — blocked raw water tubes reduce cooling capacity identically. NOTE: After replacing the heat exchanger, flush the entire system, refill with fresh coolant (the correct fluid specified in the MerCruiser Service Manual marine coolant), bleed air, and verify operating temperature.

**GUS'S DIAGNOSTIC OUTPUT:**
[Phase: PHASE_A_TRIAGE]

DIAGNOSTIC REASONING:
The symptom of gradual overheating, especially at higher RPMs, strongly suggests a few primary causes. A blown head gasket is a high probability, as the FSM states it often causes normal temperatures at low RPM but overheating above 3000 RPM due to combustion gases entering the cooling system (Source 1, 17, 34, 43). Cooling system flow restrictions, such as a defective thermostat, worn seawater pump impeller, or clogged intake, would also cause overheating, particularly under load (Source 23, 51). Finally, a faulty temperature gauge or sender could give a false reading (Source 16, 23). The initial diagnostic steps need to differentiate between these possibilities.

MECHANIC INSTRUCTIONS:
The engine overheating, especially at higher RPMs, points to a few critical areas.   Option 2: I performed the clear plastic hose test: At cruising speed (2500-3500 rpm), I observed an intermittent flow of coolant that stopped within approximately five minutes, with no continuous air bubbling. (Source 17)

### ISSUE #30: Shift Interrupt Switch Failure
**Category:** Ignition / Electrical
**Customer Complaint:** The engine starts and idles fine but won't rev above idle — it acts like it has a rev limiter that kicks in immediately.

**ANSWER KEY FIX (Ground Truth):**
Check the shift interrupt switch (also called the neutral safety/shift cutout switch). This switch prevents the engine from revving above idle unless the drive is in gear. If the switch fails or is misadjusted, it keeps the engine in idle-only mode regardless of the shift position. The switch is located on the shift plate in the engine compartment. Adjust per the MerCruiser service manual — typically there is a specific gap spec between the switch and the shift cam. Replace the switch if it has failed (the correct OEM or equivalent replacement part). Also check the shift cable adjustment — if the cable is misadjusted, the shift plate may not move far enough to trigger the switch. NOTE: On EFI engines, the ECM may also limit RPM if it doesn't see a valid signal from the shift position sensor.

**GUS'S DIAGNOSTIC OUTPUT:**
[Phase: PHASE_A_TRIAGE]

DIAGNOSTIC REASONING:
The symptom 'engine starts and idles fine but won't rev above idle' and the description 'acts like it has a rev limiter' points to a fundamental issue preventing the engine from developing power beyond idle. This could be due to a lack of ignition timing advance, insufficient fuel delivery under load, or a severe air intake issue. The FSM indicates that a faulty Ignition Control (IC) circuit or module can cause the engine to run on distributor module timing, leading to 'reduced performance' (Source 34, 56), which would feel like a rev limiter if timing isn't advancing. The Ignition Module is designed to add spark advance during acceleration (Source 7). Fuel system issues like clogged main metering jets, a faulty power valve (carbureted), or low fuel pressure (EFI/carbureted) can cause 'low top speed or lack of power' and 'poor acceleration' (Source 6, 19, 29, 33, 49). Lastly, significant vacuum leaks can also cause poor acceleration (Source 29, 32). These three areas represent distinct subsystems and are the most probable causes for the described symptom.

MECHANIC INSTRUCTIONS:
The engine is hitting a hard limit when you try to rev it. This suggests either the ignition timing isn't advancing, the fuel system can't keep up with demand, or there's a major air intake problem. We need to isolate which system is failing. Perform the following checks:

DIAGNOSTIC OPTIONS PRESENTED:
  Option 1: I connected a timing light. The timing mark stays fixed at base timing (e.g., 10° BTDC) even when I try to increase RPM.
  Option 2: I connected a timing light. The timing mark advances smoothly as RPM increases.
  Option 3: I checked fuel delivery. Fuel pressure drops significantly as I try to increase RPM (EFI), or the accelerator pump stream is weak/intermittent (carbureted).
  Option 4: I checked fuel delivery. Fuel pressure remains steady at specification (30 psi for EFI, 3-7 psi for carb) as I try to increase RPM, or the accelerator pump provides a strong, continuous stream (carbureted).
  Option 5: I sprayed for vacuum leaks. The engine RPM changes or stumbles when I spray a specific area around the intake manifold or carburetor/throttle body.

**ORIGINAL GRADE:** PARTIALLY_CORRECT
**GRADING RATIONALE:** Gus focused on broader ignition/fuel systems, but option #1 (timing fixed at base) reveals the exact symptom of a failed shift interrupt switch.

### ISSUE #31: Gimbal Bearing Failure
**Category:** Sterndrive Unit
**Customer Complaint:** There's a growling/grinding noise coming from behind the engine that gets louder when I turn the steering wheel hard to one side.

**ANSWER KEY FIX (Ground Truth):**
Replace the gimbal bearing. The gimbal bearing supports the drive shaft as it enters the outdrive and allows the drive to pivot for steering and trim. Water intrusion (from torn U-joint bellows) or lack of lubrication causes the bearing to corrode and fail. Remove the drive, remove the old bearing with a slide hammer/puller (the correct OEM or equivalent tool), and press in a new bearing (the correct OEM or equivalent replacement part) using the MerCruiser gimbal bearing driver (the correct OEM or equivalent tool). The bearing must be seated to the correct depth. After installation, check engine-to-drive alignment with the alignment tool. NOTE: By the time you hear the growling noise, the bearing is already severely damaged. A failed gimbal bearing can crack the gimbal housing ($3,000+ replacement). Always replace the bellows and bearing together — if the bellows leaked, the bearing is contaminated.

**GUS'S DIAGNOSTIC OUTPUT:**
[Phase: PHASE_A_TRIAGE]

DIAGNOSTIC REASONING:
The growling/grinding noise that intensifies with steering wheel input strongly points to the power steering system. The primary suspects are:
1.  **Low Power Steering Fluid or Air in the System:** The service manual states that 'All air must be removed, or fluid in pump may foam during operation and be discharged from pump reservoir. Foamy fluid also may cause power steering system to become spongy...' (Source 2, page 16). Low fluid or air can cause the pump to cavitate and produce noise. The manual outlines procedures for checking fluid level (Source 34, page 15) and bleeding the system (Source 2, page 16).
2.  **Faulty Power Steering Pump:** The troubleshooting section lists 'Noisy Pump' as a symptom under 'Power Steering' (Source 32, page 6). If fluid level and air are ruled out, internal pump wear or damage is a strong possibility.
3.  **Loose or Damaged Drive Belt:** A loose or worn serpentine belt driving the power steering pump can slip under load (when turning the wheel), causing noise. While often a squeal, a severe condition could manifest as a grinding sound. The manual mentions 'Drive belt slipping' as a cause for 'Squeaks or squeals' (Source 38, page 19).

MECHANIC INSTRUCTIONS:
That growling noise tied to steering input points directly to the power steering system. We need to start by checking the basics: fluid level, fluid condition, and the drive belt. Get in there and tell me what you see.

DIAGNOSTIC OPTIONS PRESENTED:
  Option 1: The power steering fluid level is low, or the fluid appears foamy/aerated.
  Option 2: The power steering fluid level is correct, and the fluid is clear with no foam.
  Option 3: The serpentine belt driving the power steering pump is loose, worn, or damaged.

**ORIGINAL GRADE:** PARTIALLY_CORRECT

### ISSUE #32: U-Joint Failure / Corrosion
**Category:** Sterndrive Unit
**Customer Complaint:** There's a metallic clunking from the drive area when shifting into gear and a vibration at all speeds.

**ANSWER KEY FIX (Ground Truth):**
Replace the U-joint assembly. The MerCruiser Alpha/Bravo uses a double U-joint (CV-style) coupling between the engine and the outdrive. Water intrusion from torn bellows corrodes the cross bearings, causing vibration and play. To check: with the drive removed, grasp the U-joint shaft and check for play in any direction — any perceptible play means replacement. The Alpha One Gen II U-joint assembly is the correct OEM or equivalent replacement part. Bravo uses a different assembly — verify by drive serial number. Replacing the aft cross bearing requires special tools and disassembly of the upper drive housing. Lightly grease cross bearings with MerCruiser cross-bearing grease (the correct fluid specified in the MerCruiser Service Manual) — if greaseable type. On non-greaseable (Perma-Lube) type, no maintenance is required. NOTE: Also check the aft yoke seal surface — the front oil seal of the outdrive rides on this machined surface. If the yoke is rusted or pitted, the seal won't hold and gear oil will leak into the bellows area.

**GUS'S DIAGNOSTIC OUTPUT:**
[Phase: PHASE_A_TRIAGE]

DIAGNOSTIC REASONING:
The metallic clunking when shifting into gear and vibration at all speeds strongly suggest an issue in the power transfer from the engine to the propeller. The primary suspects are: 
1.  **Shift System Malfunction:** Improper shift cable adjustment or insufficient remote control travel can prevent the transmission from fully engaging, leading to clunking and potential internal damage, which would then cause vibration (Source 7, 17, 26, 44, 46). The FSM explicitly states that insufficient shift cable travel will cause the transmission to slip and eventually fail (Source 7).
2.  **Drive Line Component Issues:** Problems with the propeller, propeller shaft, drive shaft, or coupler can cause both clunking (if loose or damaged) and vibration. Specifically, the FSM lists propeller shaft, propeller to shaft fit, or the propeller itself as possible causes for vibration (Source 33). A loose flywheel or coupler can also cause noise in the crankshaft area, timed to engine speed (Source 13, 30).
3.  **Transmission Internal Damage:** While less likely as an initial cause, prolonged improper shifting or drive line issues can lead to internal transmission damage, which would manifest as both clunking and vibration (Source 7).

MECHANIC INSTRUCTIONS:
The metallic clunking when shifting into gear and the vibration at all speeds point to a problem in the drivetrain, from the shift mechanism to the propeller. We need to start by checking the most common and easily verifiable issues. An improperly adjusted shift cable can cause the transmission to not fully engage, leading to clunking and eventual internal damage, which would also cause vibration. Alternatively, issues with the propeller, drive shaft, or coupler could be the root cause of both symptoms. Let's begin by checking the shift system and external drive components.

DIAGNOSTIC OPTIONS PRESENTED:
  Option 1: The shift cable adjustment is incorrect, or the remote control does not provide sufficient travel, preventing the transmission shift lever from fully engaging in forward or reverse. (Refer to FSM Section 8C-6 for Hurth or 8B-14 for Velvet Drive for adjustment procedures).
  Option 2: The propeller is visibly damaged (e.g., bent blades, chipped), loose on the shaft, or the keyway is damaged. There is also visible play or damage in the drive shaft or U-joints.
  Option 3: The shift cable adjustment appears correct, the propeller is secure and undamaged, and there is no visible damage or excessive play in the drive shaft or U-joints.

**ORIGINAL GRADE:** PARTIALLY_CORRECT
**GRADING RATIONALE:** Checked shift cable and propeller first; clunk+vibration combo strongly points to U-joint but path would eventually get there via option #3.

### ISSUE #45: Stray Current Corrosion (Electrolysis)
**Category:** Corrosion / Zincs
**Customer Complaint:** My drive is being destroyed by corrosion despite having good zincs — it's like the aluminum is dissolving.

**ANSWER KEY FIX (Ground Truth):**
Stray current corrosion (electrolysis) is caused by DC current flowing through the water from the boat's electrical system (or from shore power/neighboring boats). This is far more destructive than normal galvanic corrosion. Diagnose: with the boat in the water, measure voltage between the drive and a reference electrode (silver/silver-chloride). Readings outside manufacturer specification per the MerCruiser Service Manual indicate stray current. Systematically disconnect circuits to identify the leak source. Common sources: (1) Faulty shore power ground (galvanic isolator or isolation transformer needed). (2) Bilge pump or other submerged DC device with a ground fault. (3) Corroded battery cables or wiring. (4) Neighboring boats in the marina with electrical faults. Install a galvanic isolator on the shore power ground wire. Fix all ground faults in the DC system. Replace damaged hardware. NOTE: Stray current corrosion can destroy a $5,000+ sterndrive in a single season. If you're in a marina and your zincs last less than a month, stray current is the likely cause.

**GUS'S DIAGNOSTIC OUTPUT:**
[Phase: PHASE_A_TRIAGE]

DIAGNOSTIC REASONING:
Aluminum dissolving despite good zincs indicates a breakdown in the galvanic corrosion protection system. The primary suspects are improper antifouling paint application, which can create a galvanic couple and overwhelm the anodes, or a malfunction in the MerCathode system (if equipped) or its continuity circuit. The FSM explicitly warns against applying antifouling paint to the drive unit or too close to anodes/MerCathode, and also specifies checking the continuity circuit and MerCathode output as part of scheduled maintenance. Even 'good zincs' can be rendered ineffective if painted or if there's a stronger electrical issue at play.

MECHANIC INSTRUCTIONS:
The aluminum on your drive unit is dissolving, which points to a severe galvanic corrosion issue. While you state the zincs are good, their effectiveness can be compromised by external factors or a fault in the active corrosion protection system. We need to determine if there's an issue with antifouling paint application or the MerCathode system (if present). Describe the physical observations related to these areas.

DIAGNOSTIC OPTIONS PRESENTED:
  Option 1: The drive unit or transom assembly has antifouling paint applied, or there's less than 1 inch (26mm) unpainted area around the anodes/MerCathode.
  Option 2: The boat has a MerCathode system, and I have not recently checked its output or the continuity circuit.
  Option 3: The zincs are unpainted and have good electrical contact, and there is no MerCathode system on this boat.

**ORIGINAL GRADE:** PARTIALLY_CORRECT
**GRADING RATIONALE:** Checked MerCathode and paint interference but didn't specifically test for stray DC current with reference electrode — the critical diagnostic for electrolysis.

[Phase: PHASE_A_TRIAGE]

### ISSUE #90: Bonding System Failure
**Category:** Corrosion / Zincs
**Customer Complaint:** Individual metal components on the boat are corroding at different rates — some are fine and others are being destroyed.

**ANSWER KEY FIX (Ground Truth):**
Inspect and repair the bonding system. The bonding system connects all underwater metals (thru-hulls, shafts, drives, trim tabs) with a heavy-gauge green wire to a common ground bonded to the zinc anodes. If any connection is broken or corroded, the disconnected component loses protection. Inspect every bonding wire connection — clean to bare metal, apply dielectric grease, and reconnect. Check for continuity between all underwater metals and the main zinc anode — should be within manufacturer specification per the MerCruiser Service Manual. Replace any corroded bonding wire with new within manufacturer specification per the MerCruiser Service Manual tinned copper marine wire. NOTE: ABYC Standard E-2 covers bonding requirements. The bonding system must be included in any annual survey or maintenance inspection.

**GUS'S DIAGNOSTIC OUTPUT:**
[Phase: PHASE_A_TRIAGE]

DIAGNOSTIC REASONING:
The symptom of individual metal components corroding at different rates strongly indicates a problem with the boat's galvanic corrosion protection system. The service manual explicitly warns against improper application of antifouling paint to anodes or the MerCathode System reference electrode, stating that this will render them ineffective as galvanic corrosion inhibitors and cause corrosion damage. This suggests the sacrificial anodes or the MerCathode system are either compromised, depleted, or not functioning correctly, leading to accelerated corrosion of other metal components.

MECHANIC INSTRUCTIONS:
The varying rates of corrosion on individual metal components point directly to an issue with the boat's galvanic corrosion protection. Your first step is to physically inspect the sacrificial anodes (anodic blocks) and the MerCathode System reference electrode. You need to determine their condition and whether they are being interfered with. Pay close attention to any signs of antifouling paint on these components, as this will render them ineffective. Refer to the 'Marine Fouling' section in the General Information for context on proper antifouling paint application and anode protection (Source 15, page 1A-8).

DIAGNOSTIC OPTIONS PRESENTED:
  Option 1: The sacrificial anodes (anodic blocks) are heavily corroded or completely gone, and/or the MerCathode system appears damaged or missing.

### ISSUE #97: Tilt Lock Mechanism Failure
**Category:** Trim / Tilt System
**Customer Complaint:** The drive slams down when I release the trim button — it won't hold in the tilted-up position for trailering.

**ANSWER KEY FIX (Ground Truth):**
Check the tilt lock mechanism and the hydraulic check valve. On Alpha/Bravo drives, the tilt lock is either a mechanical latch (tilt lock lever on the side of the drive) or a hydraulic check valve in the trim pump that holds pressure. If the mechanical latch is missing or broken, replace it. If the hydraulic system won't hold, the pump's internal check valve or the trim cylinder seals are bypassing (see 'Trim Rams Leaking' entry). Also check the manual relief valve — if left open, the system can't hold pressure. NOTE: For trailering, always use the drive support/trailer lock to hold the drive up — don't rely solely on hydraulic pressure. A failed check valve while trailering can cause the drive to drop onto the road.

The provided FSM documents do not contain specific troubleshooting procedures for a trim system that fails to hold position, so this diagnosis relies on general hydraulic system principles.
